## Story 1 — Onboarding redesign: shipping the data winner over the prettier variant   ⭐
**Covers archetypes:** judgment under ambiguity · ambiguous bet / trade-off · leadership / direction
**Best for questions:** "decision driven with data", "a ship/no-ship call you made", "drive measurable impact"

- **S:** At Riverbend Apps, the onboarding/activation flow was the biggest lever on retention and I owned the experimentation roadmap for it.
- **T:** I had to decide which onboarding variant to ship and own the success metric behind the call.
- **A:** I designed a 4-arm test on the activation flow. I pre-defined D7 retention as the success metric and instrumented it. Then I made the call to ship the variant that lifted retention even though a prettier competing variant tested flat.
- **R:** D7 retention rose from 22% to 28%; I owned both the success metric and the ship decision.
- **L:** Pre-commit to the metric before you see the results, then have the spine to ship the winner over the favorite.

**The meta-move:** I shipped the highest-retention arm → the textbook move is to ship the highest-measured-lift variant → I held to a pre-defined success metric and chose retention over aesthetics, owning the call.
**Strengthen before the room:** tighten to 90s; be ready to whiteboard the 4-arm design and how you sized it (MDE) in 60s.

## Story 2 — Killing the mandatory tutorial with a holdout
**Covers archetypes:** judgment under ambiguity · disagreeing up / pushback · ambiguous bet
**Best for questions:** "decision with incomplete information", "disagreed with a stakeholder", "let data settle a debate"

- **S:** Leadership at Riverbend wanted to add a mandatory tutorial to onboarding.
- **T:** I suspected it would hurt activation and had to resolve the disagreement without just arguing.
- **A:** Instead of debating in the room, I ran a holdout experiment on the tutorial. I measured its effect on activation. Then I brought the result back as the decision input.
- **R:** The tutorial dropped activation 6%, so we killed it; the data ended the debate.
- **L:** When you disagree with leadership, run the test instead of the argument — evidence settles it cleaner than opinion.

**The meta-move:** I ran a holdout to test a leadership-favored idea → the standard path is to ship the bet or fight it in a meeting → I chose a clean causal read, accepting the time cost, so the decision was data-driven not political.
**Strengthen before the room:** add the sample size / runtime if you can recall it; otherwise ⚠️ needs a number on test duration.

## Story 3 — Reframing referral from invites-sent to activated-invitees   ⭐
**Covers archetypes:** disagreeing up / pushback · judgment (metric definition) · leadership / direction
**Best for questions:** "disagreed with leadership", "define/own a metric", "a time you pushed back on a vanity metric"

- **S:** At Cobalt Mobile (referral program), a VP wanted to optimize for invites sent.
- **T:** I believed invites-sent was a vanity metric and had to change what we optimized for.
- **A:** I argued that invites-sent didn't reflect real growth and pushed to measure activated-invitees instead. I rebuilt the referral readout around that real metric. Then I re-evaluated the variants against it.
- **R:** It changed which variants we shipped. (Referral work also drove a 15% lift in invites sent — note: that's the very metric being argued against, so lead with the metric-change outcome, not the 15%. Activated-invitees lift ⚠️ needs a number.)
- **L:** Optimize the metric that means something, not the one that's easy to move; the metric you choose decides what ships.

**The meta-move:** I switched the optimization target to activated-invitees → the easy path is to report the metric leadership asked for → I redefined the metric around real value, changing the ship decisions, at the cost of a harder-to-move number.
**Strengthen before the room:** chase down an activated-invitees number to make the result quantified; right now the result is "changed what we shipped," which is good but unquantified.

## Story 4 — Honest about the gap: slices of PM, not the whole job
**Covers archetypes:** failure with recovery / self-awareness · judgment
**Best for questions:** "a weakness / where are you still growing", "honest about a gap", "why this role is a stretch"

- **S:** Across 6 years I've made ship calls and shipped growth features, but always as the analyst/experimentation owner.
- **T:** I have to be honest about which parts of the PM job I've done and which I haven't.
- **A:** I name it directly: I've owned success metrics and ship decisions and the experimentation roadmap. I have *not* owned a product area end to end across a roadmap — prioritizing a backlog, writing full PRDs, running quarterly planning. I'm targeting this role precisely because it's experimentation-heavy (my strength) so I grow into the product-craft half on solid ground.
- **R:** ⚠️ no number — this is a self-awareness story, not an impact story; the "result" is a credible, specific growth plan.
- **L:** Know exactly which slices of the job you own cold and which you're stretching into — and pick roles where your strength carries the stretch.

**The meta-move:** I'm transparent about the roadmap/PRD/quarterly-planning gap → the tempting move is to overstate "I've basically been a PM" → I scope my claim honestly to the slices I've owned, which makes the slices I *do* claim more credible.
**Strengthen before the room:** pair this with a concrete plan for closing the gap (how you'd run your first 90 days owning a backlog) so it lands as growth, not deficiency.

## Story 5 — LTV model that reallocated $1.5M of spend
**Covers archetypes:** judgment under ambiguity · ambiguous bet / trade-off · driving business impact
**Best for questions:** "drive measurable/business impact", "a data-driven decision", "an analytical bet"

- **S:** At Cobalt Mobile I owned paid-acquisition analytics where spend was being judged on shallow metrics.
- **T:** I had to make acquisition spend pay back on real value, not surface ROAS.
- **A:** I built an LTV model for acquired users. I used it to compare channels on lifetime value rather than last-click. Then I drove a reallocation of spend toward the channels with real payback.
- **R:** The model reallocated ~$1.5M of acquisition spend.
- **L:** Model the outcome that actually matters (lifetime value), and the spend decisions follow.

**The meta-move:** I reallocated $1.5M on an LTV basis → the standard is last-click ROAS → I modeled lifetime value instead, accepting model complexity for decisions that hold up over the full customer lifetime.
**Strengthen before the room:** be ready to sketch the LTV model's inputs in 60s; add the resulting CAC/payback improvement if you can recall it (⚠️ needs a number).
