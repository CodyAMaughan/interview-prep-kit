## Story 1 — The 14-clinic onboarding playbook   ⭐
**Covers archetypes:** judgment under ambiguity · leadership/direction · structured thinking
**Best for questions:** "decision with incomplete information" · "ownership of a cross-functional outcome" · "how do you handle no playbook"

- **S:** At Summit Health Group I had to onboard 14 new clinic locations and there was no playbook — every site wanted something different.
- **T:** I owned the onboarding program end-to-end: the roadmap, the standup cadence, and getting all 14 live.
- **A:** I picked the three sites most different from each other and ran their onboarding manually. Then I wrote down what was actually common across all three. Then I turned that into a standard playbook and rolled it across the remaining sites.
- **R:** Average onboarding time dropped from 11 weeks to 7 weeks across the 14 locations.
- **L:** When there's no playbook, sample the extremes first — what survives the most-different cases is your real standard.

**The meta-move:** I owned the program → the textbook move is to standardize before scaling → I deliberately discovered the standard from the three hardest cases first, so the playbook held across all 14 instead of breaking on the edge cases.
**Strengthen before the room:** tighten to 90s; have the team size ready (⚠️ needs a number — who was on the program team?).

## Story 2 — Pushing back on the one-quarter rollout   ⭐
**Covers archetypes:** disagreeing up / pushback · judgment · structured thinking
**Best for questions:** "disagreed with leadership" · "a time you said no" · "made a case with data"

- **S:** Leadership wanted all 14 clinics onboarded in a single quarter.
- **T:** I had to either deliver an unrealistic plan or make the case for a different one.
- **A:** I ran the staffing math and showed we'd burn out the team and miss quality bars. I proposed a phased rollout of 4–5 sites per quarter instead. Then I committed to and ran the phased plan.
- **R:** The first phased cohort had far fewer escalations than a compressed plan would have produced. ⚠️ needs a number — how many fewer escalations, or vs. what baseline?
- **L:** Disagreeing up works when you bring the math, not the opinion — give leadership a credible alternative, not just a "no."

**The meta-move:** I pushed back → the easy path was to commit to the aggressive date → I chose to trade speed for quality with a staffing-backed phased plan, and owned delivering it.
**Strengthen before the room:** quantify the escalation reduction (the result is currently qualitative).

## Story 3 — Turning floor-staff pain into a ranked issue list
**Covers archetypes:** user discovery → prioritization · conflict/collaboration · translating ambiguity
**Best for questions:** "synthesize customer problems into requirements" · "how do you prioritize" · "worked between users and engineers"

- **S:** On the Frontier Freight warehouse scheduling rollout across 5 sites, floor staff kept saying "the tool is confusing" and the vendor's engineers couldn't act on that.
- **T:** I was the liaison — my job was to turn vague user pain into something engineers could actually build against.
- **A:** I sat with the floor staff and pulled specific failures out of "it's confusing." I ranked the issues by frequency and severity into a prioritized list. Then I handed the engineers the top-pain items first and tracked them to resolution.
- **R:** Scheduling errors dropped by about a third across the 5 sites.
- **L:** "It's confusing" is never a requirement — your job is to convert user pain into a ranked list someone can ship against.

**The meta-move:** I triaged user pain → the SOTA is structured discovery → I ranked by frequency-and-severity so the highest-pain fixes shipped first, which is why errors fell ~1/3.
**Strengthen before the room:** be ready to say how many issues you triaged (⚠️ needs a number) and to map this explicitly to PM discovery/backlog prioritization.

## Story 4 — Vendor consolidation that cut $220k/year
**Covers archetypes:** ownership / cost discipline · stakeholder management · trade-off
**Best for questions:** "drove a measurable result" · "negotiation / managing vendors" · "ownership"

- **S:** Summit Health Group was carrying redundant supply vendors and the supply spend was higher than it needed to be.
- **T:** I owned consolidating the vendor base without disrupting clinical supply.
- **A:** I mapped the overlapping vendors and spend. I negotiated a consolidated arrangement. Then I managed the transition so clinics weren't left short.
- **R:** Saved roughly $220k/year in supply costs.
- **L:** Cost wins come from removing redundancy without breaking the people downstream who depend on the supply.

**The meta-move:** I consolidated vendors → the standard play is lowest-price-per-line → I chose fewer-vendors-with-managed-transition to protect clinical continuity, and still captured ~$220k/yr.
**Strengthen before the room:** have the # of vendors before→after ready (⚠️ needs a number).
